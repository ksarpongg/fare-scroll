A Nation on the Move

Ghana’s transport system stretches across forests, cities, and coastlines, carrying hopes, goods, and grievances.

Greater Accra, Ghana’s most densely populated and highly trafficked region carries the daily weight of millions.

This fast-growing town sits on the edge of Accra, a transport lifeline between the capital and the Central Region. Yet it’s where a new kind of manipulation begins.

One journey, many fares.

The fare cut promised “relief". But for commuters, it’s more confusion, more cash and no change. Commercial drivers first stop 11.2km east at Old Barrier for GHC 4.50, an unassuming junction that marks the start of segmentation.

“They call it a fare cut but they have just cut the route and not the cost.”
— Commuter, Kasoa

Now, drivers insist on stopping mid route and charging a new fare. What used to be a checkpoint is now a new fare point. The segmentation begins.

A journey repackaged. From Old Barrier to Mallam junction, a short leg of a once-full trip. Another GHC 4 fare. Another delay. Another hustle.

A brief pause and another payment. Commuters are told it’s a “different route". Another GHC 4 again. The vehicle is the same. The story isn’t.

No sign, no law. There’s no notice, no official fare list, just the voice of the mate shouting a new route.

At Kaneshie bus stop, the journey nears its end, but the receipts stack higher. A market of motion, with prices set on whim.

It’s still the same trotro, same driver. But fares keep climbing.

The final stop. What was once a GH¢10 ride is now GH¢15. Same road. Different rules. And the commuter pays the price.
